Risks and Challenges examples

Risk 1: 'FOMO' & Mental Health

TBuilding on what was mentioned earlier, smartphones become increasingly popular among adolescents, and with social media use on the rise in this age group, a pressing concern has emerged: the Fear of Missing Out, or ‘FOMO’.
This constant need to remain in the loop can escalate anxiety and lead to feelings of inadequacy, loneliness, or depression.

Smartphones offer a vast amount of information, making them more than just connection tools to see what their peers are up to.
In conjunction with the internet and its wide reach, they also serve a broader purpose, allowing users to convene information and feel more in control of their surroundings (Casale et al., 2022).
Xiao et al (2023) and Casale et al (2022) both add to this by closely associating FOMO to smartphone use among adolescents.
However, Xiao et al (2023) further add that constantly checking their smartphones to see what they’re missing out on, can further intensify their existing feelings of FOMO.
Both sources also elaborate on how the increased feelings of FOMO can drive adolescents to use their smartphones as a coping mechanism to alleviate their anxiety about missing out.
However, Xiao et al delve deeper into this, suggesting that this can lead to a repetitive cycle, potentially spiraling into compulsive smartphone use, which in turn, can have profound implications for their mental health - including existing anxieties and loneliness.

Risk 2: Stress, Anxiety & Depression

The risks associated with smartphone addiction, particularly its effects on the mental and emotional health of adolescents, are notable.
Excessive and problematic smartphone usage can harm interpersonal relationships and is linked with various psychological and behavioral issues, including anxiety, depression, sleep disorders, and suicidal thoughts (Yang et al., 2018).
Additionally, reliance on smartphones can disrupt daily routines, alter personal relationships, and negatively impact health and overall contentment (Yang et al., 2018).
Stress is suggested tobe a driving factor behind the overuse of smartphones, impacting cognitive and emotional reactions.
The way individuals perceive stress influences their cognitive processes, causing them to use smartphones as a form of stress relief Xiao et al (2023).
Furthermore, Yang et al state that adolescents, due to their increased risk of impulsivity, might be more prone to problematic smartphone use during stressful times.

In contrast, Coyne et al (2018) discuss that during adolescence, consistent social media use through smartphones is common, and usually doesn't hinder social development or functionality into early adulthood. While smartphone overuse has its downsides, not all usage is harmful.
Finding a balance between mindful use and its role in teen life is important.
